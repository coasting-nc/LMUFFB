### 1. The Physics Timeline: Braking-Induced Oversteer
When you brake hard, the rear gets light, a rear tire locks, and the car snaps sideways. Here is the exact chronological order of the physics signals generated by the game engine:

#### Phase A: The Vulnerability (Pre-Loss)
*   **Longitudinal Acceleration (G-Force):** Spikes immediately. Weight transfers to the front. The rear tires lose normal load (N). 
*   *Status:* **Leading Indicator of Vulnerability.** The car hasn't lost grip yet, but the conditions for a spin are set.

#### Phase B: The Lockup (Grip Loss)
*   **Wheel Angular Deceleration (`d(Rotation)/dt`):** The brake torque exceeds the tire's grip, and the wheel stops spinning *faster* than the car is slowing down.
*   **Longitudinal Slip Ratio:** The tire begins to slide across the pavement.
*   *Status:* **Absolute Leading Indicators of Lockup.** (Note: Our engine already uses Wheel Deceleration in the `calculate_lockup_vibration` function for "Predictive Lockup").

#### Phase C: The Step-Out (Rotation Begins)
*   **Yaw Acceleration (`mLocalRotAccel.y`):** Because the rear tires lost grip, the car's momentum pushes the rear end sideways. The chassis *begins* to rotate.
*   *Status:* **Absolute Leading Indicator of Oversteer.** This happens the exact millisecond the rear steps out.

#### Phase D: The Slide (Mid-Corner)
*   **Rear Slip Angle (`m_grip_diag.rear_slip_angle`):** The rear tires are now traveling at an angle relative to the car's heading.
*   **Yaw Rate (`mLocalRot.y`):** The car is now visibly rotating.
*   *Status:* **Mid-Indicators.** You are now actively in a slide.

#### Phase E: The Roll (The Lagging Consequence)
*   **Lateral Acceleration (Lateral Gs):** Because the car is rotating, its velocity vector changes, generating lateral G-forces.
*   **Lateral Load Transfer (`LatLoadNorm`):** The lateral G-forces act on the car's Center of Gravity, causing the chassis to roll on its suspension, compressing the outside springs and unloading the inside springs.
*   *Status:* **Lagging Indicators.** 

### 2. Why "Lateral Load Under Braking" will feel bad
Based on the timeline above, **Lateral Load is a lagging signal**. It is the *result* of the roll, which is the *result* of the lateral Gs, which is the *result* of the yaw, which is the *result* of the grip loss.

If we create a "Lateral Load Under Braking" effect with zero smoothing and high strength:
1.  You brake hard. The rear locks.
2.  The car starts to step out (Yaw Acceleration). You feel nothing from this new effect yet.
3.  About 100-200 milliseconds later, the chassis finally rolls onto the outside springs.
4.  Suddenly, the Lateral Load spikes, and the wheel violently jerks in your hands.
5.  Because it's 200ms late, your brain will perceive this jerk not as a warning, but as a delayed, unnatural pendulum swing. It will likely cause you to over-correct and crash.

### 3. The Better Solution: "Load-Sensitive Yaw Kick"
You asked: *"Consider if longitudinal load could also be a useful addition to this effect under braking."*

This is the golden ticket. Longitudinal Load is the *cause* of the instability, and Yaw Acceleration is the *earliest warning* of the spin. If we combine them, we get exactly what you want.

Instead of a new Lateral Load effect, we should create a **Braking Oversteer Multiplier** applied to the existing **Yaw Kick**.

**The Logic:**
When you are braking hard (high Longitudinal G), the rear tires are unloaded. If the car experiences Yaw Acceleration during this specific time, it is almost certainly a snap-oversteer event caused by rear lockup or lift-off oversteer. 

We can dynamically boost the Yaw Kick force based on the braking G-force.

**Pseudo-code idea:**
```cpp
// 1. Calculate normal Yaw Kick
double base_yaw_kick = -1.0 * processed_yaw * m_sop_yaw_gain * BASE_NM_YAW_KICK;

// 2. Calculate Braking Vulnerability (0.0 to 1.0)
// If we are braking at 1G or more, vulnerability is high.
double braking_g = std::max(0.0, m_accel_z_smoothed / GRAVITY_MS2);
double braking_vulnerability = std::clamp(braking_g, 0.0, 1.0);

// 3. Apply a "Braking Oversteer Boost" (e.g., a user setting from 0% to 200%)
double braking_boost_factor = 1.0 + (braking_vulnerability * m_braking_yaw_boost);

// 4. Final Yaw Force
ctx.yaw_force = base_yaw_kick * braking_boost_factor;
```

### Why this is superior:
1.  **Zero Latency:** Yaw Acceleration happens instantly. You will feel the wheel counter-steer the *exact millisecond* the rear tire breaks traction.
2.  **Context Aware:** It only boosts the kick when you are on the brakes (or engine braking). If you get a yaw spike while accelerating, it behaves normally.
3.  **Builds Muscle Memory:** It trains your brain to associate "heavy brake pedal + sudden steering lightness/kick" with an imminent spin, allowing you to catch it before the chassis even has time to roll.
