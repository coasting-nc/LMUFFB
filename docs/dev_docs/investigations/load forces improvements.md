### 1. Longitudinal Load: Multiplier vs. Additive Addendum
**Verdict: It MUST remain a multiplier.**

If you look at `src/FFBEngine.cpp` (around line 360), `dynamic_weight_factor` is currently applied as a multiplier to the base steering torque:
`double output_force = (base_input * gain_to_apply) * dw_factor_applied * grip_factor_applied;`

**Why this is physically correct:**
The force you feel in a steering wheel is primarily **Aligning Torque**. Aligning torque is generated by the lateral friction of the tire acting on the pneumatic trail (the distance between the steering axis and the center of the tire's contact patch). 
* If you brake in a straight line, weight shifts forward. The normal load on the front tires increases. 
* However, because you are driving straight, there is no lateral force. 
* If we made longitudinal load an *additive* effect (`output_force = base_input + dynamic_weight`), braking in a straight line would cause the steering wheel to magically turn itself left or right (depending on the sign). 
* By keeping it a **multiplier**, braking in a straight line multiplies a base lateral force of `0.0` by `2.0`, resulting in `0.0` (the wheel stays straight). But the moment you turn the wheel even slightly, that multiplied weight makes the wheel feel significantly heavier to turn. This perfectly mimics a real steering rack.

### 2. Review of Existing Load-Scaled Effects
**Verdict: Highly robust, but we can improve two effects.**

Currently, your codebase uses a brilliant "Soft-Knee Compression" algorithm (around line 280) to create `ctx.texture_load_factor` and `ctx.brake_load_factor`. This prevents FFB clipping when the car bottoms out.
* **Road Texture & Slide Texture:** Scaled by `texture_load_factor`. (Correct: A heavily loaded tire transmits more high-frequency vibration).
* **Lockup Vibration:** Scaled by `brake_load_factor`. (Correct: A locked tire under heavy braking judders violently).
* **Rear Aligning Torque:** Scaled by `ctx.avg_rear_load`. (Correct: Rear lateral force scales with rear normal load).

**What needs fixing:**
**Scrub Drag** (in `calculate_road_texture`) is currently a constant force:
`ctx.scrub_drag_force = drag_dir * m_scrub_drag_gain * (double)BASE_NM_SCRUB_DRAG * fade;`
* *The Fix:* Scrubbing a heavily loaded tire (e.g., understeer while braking) creates vastly more steering column drag than scrubbing a light tire. You should multiply this by the load factor:
  ```cpp
  ctx.scrub_drag_force = drag_dir * m_scrub_drag_gain * (double)BASE_NM_SCRUB_DRAG * fade * ctx.texture_load_factor;
  ```

### 3. Should Lateral Load include Rear Tires?
**Verdict: YES. This is a fantastic observation.**

Currently, your `lat_load_norm` only looks at the front tires: `(fl_load - fr_load) / total_load`. 

Because this variable is used to calculate the **Seat of the Pants (SoP)** effect, it is supposed to represent what the driver's body feels (the global chassis roll), *not* just what the steering rack feels. 

If you drive a car with a very stiff rear anti-roll bar (like a FWD TCR car or a Porsche 911), the rear tires will transfer load much faster than the front tires. Sometimes the inside rear tire will even lift off the ground. If you only monitor the front tires, the FFB will completely miss this crucial chassis information.

**The Fix (in `calculate_sop_lateral`):**
Change the calculation to look at the entire car's load transfer:
```cpp
    // 2. Global Normalized Lateral Load Transfer (Chassis Roll)
    double fl_load = data->mWheel[0].mTireLoad;
    double fr_load = data->mWheel[1].mTireLoad;
    double rl_load = data->mWheel[2].mTireLoad;
    double rr_load = data->mWheel[3].mTireLoad;
    
    if (ctx.frame_warn_load) {
        fl_load = calculate_kinematic_load(data, 0);
        fr_load = calculate_kinematic_load(data, 1);
        rl_load = calculate_kinematic_load(data, 2);
        rr_load = calculate_kinematic_load(data, 3);
    }
    
    double left_load = fl_load + rl_load;
    double right_load = fr_load + rr_load;
    double total_load = left_load + right_load;
    
    double lat_load_norm = (total_load > 1.0) ? (left_load - right_load) / total_load : 0.0;
    
    // Apply Locked-Center Hermite Spline (from previous optimization)
    lat_load_norm = std::clamp(lat_load_norm, -1.0, 1.0);
    double k = (double)m_lat_load_rolloff;
    double abs_x = std::abs(lat_load_norm);
    lat_load_norm = lat_load_norm * (1.0 + k * abs_x - k * (abs_x * abs_x));
```
*This will make the car feel much more "connected" as a single rigid body, rather than just feeling the front axle.*

### 4. Other places to use Load Forces
**Verdict: Wheel Spin Vibration.**

Currently, in `calculate_wheel_spin`, the vibration amplitude is calculated as:
`double amp = severity * m_spin_gain * (double)BASE_NM_SPIN_VIBRATION;`

If a car crests a hill (like the Nürburgring Flugplatz) and the rear tires lose load and spin up, the vibration transmitted through the chassis should be relatively light. If the car is doing a burnout on a flat, heavily loaded surface, the vibration should be violent.

**The Fix:**
We can approximate a rear load factor by comparing the current rear load to the static front load (since we don't track static rear load, and they are usually within 40-60% of each other anyway).
```cpp
    // Inside calculate_wheel_spin
    double current_rear_load = (data->mWheel[2].mTireLoad + data->mWheel[3].mTireLoad) / 2.0;
    // Normalize against our known static front load, clamped for safety
    double rear_load_factor = std::clamp(current_rear_load / (m_static_front_load + 1.0), 0.2, 2.0);
    
    double amp = severity * m_spin_gain * (double)BASE_NM_SPIN_VIBRATION * rear_load_factor;
```

### 5. Should we add NEW effects based on Load?
**Verdict: You actually already have the best one, it's just hidden in plain sight!**

A common request in sim racing is a **"Vertical G / Heave"** effect—feeling the car compress into the track at the bottom of Eau Rouge, or get light over a crest.

Because your `Dynamic Weight` multiplier is based on `ctx.avg_front_load` (which is derived directly from the game's `mTireLoad` or `mSuspForce`), **it already includes vertical G-forces and aerodynamic downforce!** 
* When you hit a dip in the track, the suspension compresses, `avg_front_load` spikes, and your `dynamic_weight_factor` multiplies the steering weight. 
* When you crest a hill, the load drops, and the steering goes light.

You don't need to write a new effect for this. Your current architecture handles it elegantly and physically accurately through the existing Dynamic Weight system. 

### Summary of Recommended Actions:
1. Keep Longitudinal Load (Dynamic Weight) as a **multiplier**.
2. Update **Lateral Load (SoP)** to use `(Left_Total - Right_Total) / Car_Total`.
3. Multiply **Scrub Drag** by `ctx.texture_load_factor`.
4. Multiply **Wheel Spin** by a calculated `rear_load_factor`.
